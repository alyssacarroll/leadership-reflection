About me

Before college, I had never left the United States. By the time I graduated from Christopher Newport University, I had stood in six countries, restored a 19th-century Irish military fort, seen Olivia Rodrigo perform in Lisbon, and navigated a foreign airport completely alone — all while figuring out what it really means to lead.

This project reflects on two international experiences that shaped me more than any classroom could: my study abroad through LDSP 320: Cross-Cultural Leadership in the Netherlands, France, Luxembourg, Belgium, and Switzerland (with a free weekend in Portugal), and my service trip to Ireland with PLP. Together, they pushed me outside every comfort zone I had — and showed me that leadership, at its core, is about showing up for others even when it's hard, uncertain, or exhausting.

Key Areas of Growth

  • Other Perspectives

    Traveling abroad for the first time forced me to see the world through other people's eyes. Whether it was understanding why locals in Ireland were genuinely surprised that young Americans would volunteer to do hard physical labor, or simply feeling grateful that so many Europeans spoke English when I couldn't speak their language, I learned that perspective-taking isn't just an academic concept. It's something you feel.

  • Service

    Before Ireland, service was something I did to meet a requirement. After Ireland, it became something I wanted to do. Working alongside older local volunteers who had every reason to be tired — but weren't — changed the way I understand what it means to give back. Reaching my required service hours on that trip freed me to ask a different question: not "how many hours do I need?" but "where can I actually help?"

  • Resiliency

    I had planned everything out. I knew exactly which bus to take from the Dublin airport to my hotel. And then I got there and had absolutely no idea what I was doing. That moment — standing in an unfamiliar airport, alone, trying not to panic — taught me more about resilience than any assignment ever could. I asked strangers for help. They helped. I figured it out. That's leadership too.

  • Transformational Leadership

    Both experiences asked me to grow — not just in skill, but in mindset. I left the Netherlands thinking differently about how organizations and cultures function. I left Ireland thinking differently about why I serve. These weren't small shifts. They were the kind of changes that stay with you.

Key Experiences

  • LDSP 320: Netherlands

    Six countries. New friends. Organization visits, conversations with local leaders, and cultural experiences I never expected. And one very memorable free weekend in Lisbon, Portugal — where I saw Olivia Rodrigo in concert and remembered that planning something yourself, pulling it off, and celebrating it is a form of leadership too.

  • Service in Ireland

    At Camden Fort Meagher, I worked alongside older Irish volunteers who were out-working all of us and couldn't understand why a group of American students would fly across the Atlantic to do manual labor. Honestly? Their confusion made me think harder about my answer. And the answer I found changed how I think about service entirely.

Final Reflection

What Two Trips Across the Atlantic Taught Me About Leading

I want to start with something embarrassing: I almost missed my train in Amsterdam.

We had a plan. My friend Hannah and I had researched the route from Maastricht to our hotel in Lisbon and back, and had it all mapped out on our phones. After getting off the plane in Amsterdam, and (quite literally) running through the airport, we made it to the platform with minutes to spare. But then we had no idea which train to get on. We asked multiple strangers at the platform which train this was, all had differring answers. Right before the train doors closed, Hannah asked someone on the train if this would take us to Maastricht. He said yes and we got on as the doors were literally closing on us. The train left with us on it. We made it.

That moment — standing on a train platform in Amsterdam, completely uncertain and having to rely on the kindness of people I'd never met — turned out to be one of the most important leadership experiences of my time at CNU. Not because we handled it gracefully, but because we handled it. We asked for help. We stayed calm enough to think. And I learned that leading yourself through uncertainty is the foundation for leading anyone else.

Both of my international experiences — LDSP 320 across Europe and PLP's service trip to Ireland — pushed me in this way. They put me in situations I couldn't control and asked me to show up anyway.

Other Perspectives: Feeling Grateful for Something I'd Never Thought About

Before my study abroad, I'd never considered what it might feel like to be somewhere and not speak the language. When I arrived in the Netherlands for LDSP 320, one of my first reactions was genuine gratitude that people spoke English. I hadn't thought to be grateful for that before. Being in a foreign country, surrounded by people who could have easily chosen not to accommodate me, made me feel grateful in a way I hadn't before.

According to the Student Leadership Competency framework, Other Perspectives asks leaders to "truly consider other opinions, experiences, and outlooks," not just intellectually, but in ways that help them "relate to and appreciate others more." I thought I understood this competency. But understanding it in a classroom and feeling it in a foreign country are completely different things.

Visiting organizations and meeting local leaders throughout the Netherlands, France, Belgium, Luxembourg, and Switzerland added another layer. I observed how different cultures approach structure, communication, and authority. Leaders I met didn't lead the way I'd been taught to expect. Some were more collaborative, some more hierarchical, some more informal. None were wrong. All of them made me examine my own assumptions about what good leadership looks like.

This connects directly to Transformational Leadership theory, which describes leaders who motivate growth by challenging assumptions and inspiring others to think beyond their current perspectives. Being on the receiving end of that kind of cultural challenge, having my assumptions disrupted simply by being present in someone else's world, made me a more empathetic and self-aware leader.

Service: From Obligation to Choice

The Ireland trip completed my required PLP service hours. I want to be honest about that, because what happened after matters more: for the first time, service became something I chose rather than something I owed.

At Camden Fort Meagher, we spent days doing hard physical restoration work on a historic 19th-century military fort. We also helped with environmental cleanup in downtown Cork. The work was genuinely demanding — cleaning, hauling, clearing. What I didn't expect was who I'd be doing it next to.

The local Irish volunteers were older and they were outworking us. They were also baffled by us. More than one person asked some version of "why would you come all this way to do this?" I was happy just being able to help.

Dr. Seemiller's framework describes Service as essential to "creating and maintaining thriving communities," noting that "leaders model commitment and engagement." Those older volunteers modeled something for me. Their dedication — to a place, to a piece of history, to their environment — wasn't performative. It was just real. And it made me want to match it.

Servant Leadership theory, developed by Robert Greenleaf, holds that the most effective leaders begin with the genuine desire to serve others first. Before Ireland, I understood this in theory. After Ireland, I understood it in practice. Serving alongside people who had nothing to gain from my presence — and who chose to show up anyway — reshaped what I think leadership actually looks like at its best.

I also noticed how environmentally conscious the Irish volunteers were. There was a care for the land and the community that felt instinctive rather than performative. That mindset — of stewardship, of thinking beyond yourself — is something I'm still carrying.

The Part Nobody Plans For

I'd be leaving something out if I didn't mention Portugal.

During our free weekend on the LDSP 320 trip, a friend and I planned our own few days in Lisbon. We researched, booked, budgeted, and navigated it ourselves. We also saw Olivia Rodrigo in concert, which I will not pretend was not a highlight of my life. But beyond the concert, the experience of planning something from scratch in a foreign country — managing money, figuring out transportation, problem-solving in real time — was its own leadership lesson. Collaboration, decision-making, responding to ambiguity: all of it showed up in the space of a weekend.

The moments you don't plan are often the ones that teach you the most.

After CNU

What Comes Next

In the fall, I'll be starting a Master's program in Engineering at Virginia Tech, on the Integrated Project Design track. That means working with a team to complete a project from conception to completion — and it means everything I've learned about collaboration, perspective-taking, and showing up for people I don't yet know will matter from day one.


How PLP Prepared Me for This

Both of my international experiences asked me to collaborate with strangers — in airport terminals, on Irish hillforts, in European conference rooms, on Lisbon street corners. I learned that building trust quickly is a skill. I learned that asking for help is not weakness. I learned that the people around you — teammates, locals, volunteers, fellow students — will often surprise you with their generosity if you're willing to be open to it.

The Integrated Project Design path at Virginia Tech will ask me to do exactly this, just in an engineering context. I'm not intimidated by that. I'm ready for it. I know what it feels like to be in an unfamiliar situation, not know the answer, and figure it out anyway.


A Commitment to Service Beyond the Hours

I met my required service hours in Ireland. And then something shifted. I don't want service to be something I did in college to fulfill a program requirement. I want it to be part of how I move through the world — finding meaningful ways to contribute, even when it's hard, even when the people I'm serving don't fully understand why I'm there.

Those older volunteers at Camden Fort Meagher showed me what that looks like. They showed up because the place mattered to them and they had something to give. That's the kind of leader — and person — I want to be after CNU.
