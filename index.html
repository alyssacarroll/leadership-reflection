About me

Prior to college, I had never left the United States. By the time I graduated from Christopher Newport University, I had visited six countries, contributed to the restoration of a 19th-century Irish military fort, and navigated foreign travel independently for the first time. None of that was part of my original plan, but each experience became foundational to my development as a leader.

This reflection examines two international experiences that significantly shaped my understanding of leadership: studying abroad through LDSP 320: Cross-Cultural Leadership in the Netherlands, France, Luxembourg, Belgium, and Switzerland (including a self-planned weekend in Portugal), and a service trip to Ireland with PLP. I entered both programs with the intention to grow. I returned having done so, though often in ways I did not anticipate.

Key Areas of Growth

  • Other Perspectives

    Prior to traveling abroad, I had not considered what it means to benefit from widespread English fluency in non-English-speaking countries. Being immersed in environments where I could not communicate in the local language required me to engage with other perspectives in a way that classroom learning alone had not. This competency developed not through instruction, but through direct experience.

  • Service

    My motivations for the Ireland trip included fulfilling PLP service hour requirements, a desire to help others, and an interest in international travel. What I did not anticipate was that completing those hours would feel like a beginning rather than an endpoint. Working alongside dedicated local volunteers at Camden Fort Meagher shifted my understanding of service from an obligation to a genuine commitment.

  • Resiliency

    I arrived in Dublin with a detailed plan for reaching my hotel from the airport. That plan did not hold up in practice. Navigating an unfamiliar transit system alone, having to ask multiple strangers for guidance, was uncomfortable but instructive. Adapting in real time without access to familiar support systems was one of the most practical leadership lessons of either trip.

  • Transformational Leadership

    Both experiences produced shifts in how I think, not only in what I know. I returned from Europe with a broader understanding of how culture shapes leadership practice. I returned from Ireland with a fundamentally different relationship to service. These reflect the kind of perspective and values transformation that characterizes transformational leadership.

Key Experiences

  • LDSP 320: Netherlands

    Six countries, many new relationships, site visits to organizations, and conversations with local leaders whose approaches to leadership differed substantially from what I had studied. The trip also included a self-planned free weekend in Lisbon, Portugal, where my friend Hannah and I managed all logistics independently, including attending an Olivia Rodrigo concert, which became one of the most memorable experiences of my college years.

  • Service in Ireland

    I traveled to Ireland motivated by a genuine interest in international service, a love of traveling abroad, and the need to fulfill PLP service hour requirements. What I did not anticipate was working alongside Irish volunteers who were decades older, remarkably committed, and genuinely puzzled by why American students would travel so far to do manual labor. That question stayed with me long after the trip ended.

Final Reflection

We Almost Got Stuck in Amsterdam

The departure displays at the Amsterdam airport were malfunctioning. The boards were not showing the correct trains, and my friend Hannah and I had a connection to make. We asked multiple people on the platform which train it was and received conflicting information each time. With the doors about to close, Hannah asked a passenger already on board whether the train was bound for Maastricht. He confirmed it was. We boarded as the doors were (literally) closing on us.

We made it. Looking back, that moment captures something important about leadership that I had not fully understood before: the ability to make a reasonable decision under uncertainty, seek input from available sources, and commit to a course of action without complete information. It is a skill, and it turned out to be one I would need repeatedly across both international trips.

Both of my international PLP experiences, LDSP 320 across Europe and the service trip to Ireland, placed me in situations where my plans did not hold and I had to adapt. This reflection examines what I learned from those experiences and how they connect to my growth as a leader.

Other Perspectives: Recognizing What I Had Taken for Granted

One of the first things I noticed upon arriving in the Netherlands was the extent to which people accommodated English speakers. I had not anticipated feeling grateful for that. Prior to this experience, I had never considered the privilege embedded in being an English speaker traveling in non-English-speaking countries. People were gracious and helpful in ways I had not earned, and recognizing that was an early and significant shift in perspective.

According to the Student Leadership Competency framework, Other Perspectives asks leaders to genuinely consider the experiences and outlooks of others in ways that change how they relate to and engage with the world. I had studied this competency in coursework and believed I understood it. What I found abroad was that there is a meaningful difference between intellectual understanding and experiential understanding.

Visiting organizations and engaging with local professionals across the Netherlands, France, Belgium, Luxembourg, and Switzerland deepened this growth further. I encountered leadership styles that differed substantially from those I had studied, ranging from highly collaborative and informal to more structured and hierarchical, and all of them were effective in their contexts. This challenged my assumption that good leadership has a recognizable, universal form. What I had understood as effective leadership was, in large part, simply familiar leadership.

This experience connects directly to Transformational Leadership theory, which centers on leaders who challenge existing assumptions and inspire growth beyond current ways of thinking. I did not encounter this through a professor or a formal exercise. I encountered it by being present in environments that operated differently than anything I had known. That kind of learning is difficult to replicate in a classroom setting.

Service: From Obligation to Genuine Commitment

I will be transparent about my initial motivations for the Ireland trip: I went because I enjoy international travel, I wanted to contribute to meaningful work, and I needed to fulfill PLP service hour requirements. All three of those factors were present. What I did not anticipate was that completing those requirements would mark the beginning of a different relationship with service rather than the conclusion of one.

At Camden Fort Meagher, our group spent several days doing physical restoration work on a 19th-century military fort, as well as environmental cleanup efforts in downtown Cork City. The work was more physically demanding than I had expected, but also much more rewarding. What gave the experience its deeper meaning was the community of local volunteers we worked alongside.

Many of the Irish volunteers were significantly older than our group, and they were outpacing us. Several of them asked, in various ways, why a group of American university students would travel across the Atlantic to do this kind of manual labor. I did not have a satisfying answer in the moment. After reflecting on it, I believe the answer is this: the work mattered to them, and I had the capacity to help. That felt sufficient.

Dr. Seemiller's framework describes the competency of Service as integral to creating and sustaining thriving communities, noting that leaders model commitment and engagement. The volunteers I worked with demonstrated that modeling in a way no reading had. Their presence was not performative or resume-driven. They showed up because the place mattered to them. That example shifted something in how I understand what service actually looks like.

Robert Greenleaf's Servant Leadership theory holds that effective leadership originates in the genuine desire to serve others, prioritizing the needs of those being led above all else. Before Ireland, I could articulate that principle. After Ireland, I had experienced it. The volunteers modeled servant leadership not through formal authority, but through sustained, unpretentious dedication to something larger than themselves.

I also observed how deeply embedded environmental stewardship was in the culture of the local volunteers. Care for the land was not framed as activism or a special concern; it appeared to be simply how they understood their responsibility to their community. That orientation is something I hope to carry forward.

The Value of the Unplanned: Portugal

During the free weekend built into the LDSP 320 trip, Hannah and I planned and executed an independent stay in Lisbon, Portugal. We researched accommodations, managed our own budgets, navigated an unfamiliar city, and handled logistics that arose unexpectedly throughout the weekend. We also saw Olivia Rodrigo in concert.

Beyond the personal enjoyment, that weekend required the application of several leadership competencies simultaneously: collaboration, decision-making, financial management, and responding to ambiguity. It reinforced something I have come to believe more firmly through both trips: some of the most effective leadership development occurs outside of structured programming, in moments that require you to figure things out without a framework already in place.

After CNU

What's Next

This fall, I will begin a Master's program in Engineering at Virginia Tech on the Integrated Project Design track. The program is structured around collaborative, team-based project work taken from inception through completion. That structure closely mirrors the kinds of collaboration and adaptability I developed through PLP's international programming.


How These Experiences Apply

The skill I practiced most consistently across both international trips was working productively with people I did not yet know, asking for help when needed, establishing trust quickly, and adapting when circumstances changed. Those situations arose in the Amsterdam airport, at the Dublin bus terminal, at Camden Fort Meagher, and throughout Lisbon. Each one required me to function effectively without a complete picture of what was happening.

The Integrated Project Design track at Virginia Tech will ask me to do precisely this in an engineering context: collaborate closely with a team of people I have not yet met on a project with real stakes. I feel prepared for that in a way I would not have prior to these experiences. Not because I have all the answers, but because I have learned to work well without them.


A Commitment to Service Beyond the Requirements

I completed my required PLP service hours in Ireland. For the first time, that completion felt like a starting point rather than a conclusion. My intention is for service to remain a meaningful part of how I engage with the communities I am part of after graduation, not as a structured obligation, but as a genuine practice of showing up for others when I have something to contribute.

The volunteers at Camden Fort Meagher offered a clear model of what that can look like. They were present because the place mattered to them and they had the ability to help. That is the standard I want to hold myself to going forward.
